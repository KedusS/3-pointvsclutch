Data Analysis · WNBA 2006–2025

The Clutch Question

Do WNBA players actually perform better when the game is on the line — and has that changed over two decades of play?

20 seasons of play-by-play data  ·  Clutch = final 5 min, score within 5 pts

What is "clutch time"?

The NBA defines clutch time as the final five minutes of regulation or overtime when the score is within five points — a definition adopted here for the WNBA. During this window, every possession counts and the pressure is maximal.

To measure whether players rise to that pressure, we compare their shooting percentage during clutch time against their baseline — every shot taken outside of those moments. The difference is their clutch delta.

A positive delta means a player shoots better when the game is close and the clock is short. A negative delta means they shoot worse. We call players with a delta above +5 pp "Clutch," below −5 pp "Anti-clutch."

A league trending toward average

From 2008 to 2018, the average player was usually above baseline in clutch situations, though the year-to-year pattern is noisy. Since 2019, the trend has been more volatile, with several seasons dipping into negative territory.

The 2020 bubble season stands out as a dramatic outlier: the average player shot nearly 5 percentage points worse in clutch time compared to their baseline. The compressed schedule, neutral-site environment, and psychological strain of the bubble likely disrupted late-game execution for everyone.

What explains the broader flattening? One possibility: the game itself has changed. As the WNBA has adopted the three-pointer more aggressively, clutch shot selection has shifted in ways that may affect overall accuracy.

The three-point effect

Clutch shot selection has tracked the broader WNBA shift toward three-pointers. In 2006, about 17% of clutch attempts were threes. By 2025, that share had grown to over 23%.

In clutch situations, teams increasingly reach for the three — perhaps rationally, since a made three can erase a two-possession deficit instantly. But three-pointers are lower-percentage shots than twos and free throws, so shot mix is an important context for interpreting clutch delta. The relationship is exploratory, not proof that threes cause better or worse clutch performance.
